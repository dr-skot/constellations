# Other Sky Traditions

The modern 88 constellations are entirely a Western lineage — Babylonian, Greek, Arabic, European — globalized through the IAU in 1922. But many cultures developed their own ways of organizing the sky.

## China
China had the most developed independent system. The sky was divided into the **Three Enclosures** around the pole and **Twenty-Eight Mansions** along the celestial equator (analogous to the zodiac but with 28 divisions). Beyond those, about 283 smaller asterisms — tending to be much smaller groupings than Western constellations, and reflecting the imperial bureaucracy: the Emperor's throne, government offices, market stalls, armies. Completely independent of the Western tradition until Jesuit missionaries brought European astronomy in the 17th century.

## Polynesia
Polynesian navigators had sophisticated star knowledge for open-ocean navigation — not constellation figures per se, but a **star compass** dividing the horizon into houses where specific stars rose and set. Stars were organized by their utility for wayfinding, not by mythological figures. The Pleiades were important across nearly all Pacific cultures.

## The Inca
The Inca recognized **dark constellations** — shapes formed by dark patches in the Milky Way (a llama, a fox, a toad, a serpent) rather than by connecting bright stars. A fundamentally different way of seeing the sky.

## The Maya
The Maya had detailed astronomical knowledge tied to their calendar, tracking Venus and other bodies with precision, though their "constellation" system is less well documented.

## North America
Many traditions. The Pawnee had particularly detailed star knowledge. The Lakota had their own constellation figures. Various cultures recognized the Pleiades, Orion's belt, and the Big Dipper under different names and stories.

## Aboriginal Australia
Aboriginal Australian astronomy may be the oldest continuous astronomical tradition. Like the Inca, Aboriginal Australians recognized **dark constellations** — most famously the **Emu in the Sky**, where the Coalsack Nebula near Crux forms the emu's head and the dark lanes of the Milky Way stretching toward Scorpius form the neck and body. The emu's position across seasons signaled when emu eggs were available — practical astronomy tied to the land.

## Africa
The least well documented due to oral traditions, but rich star lore existed. The San people of southern Africa, various East African cultures, and others all had star names and groupings. Egyptian astronomy (decans, star clocks) was its own system and influenced Greek astronomy to some degree, though less directly than Babylonian.

---

**Note:** The 88 constellations are a European system that became the global standard through colonialism and the dominance of Western science. The IAU didn't consider alternative traditions when they standardized in 1922 — they were codifying a specific European lineage.
